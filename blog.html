Mastering the Airwaves: A Deep Dive into WISP RF Technology

Working as an Assistant Network Engineer on the RF Team at Amber IT, I've gained extensive experience in managing complex Wireless Internet Service Provider (WISP) infrastructure. Our network leverages best-in-class solutions from Cambium Networks, Ubiquiti, and Mimosa to deliver high-speed, reliable connectivity in challenging environments.

The Foundation: Key RF Technologies

1. OFDM & MIMO (The Speed Boost)

All our modern WISP gear utilizes OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing) to make signals robust against interference, and MIMO (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output) to use multiple antennas for simultaneous data transmission. This combination significantly boosts spectral efficiency and overall throughput.

2. TDMA (The Reliability Engine)

Our PtMP links (Cambium ePMP, Ubiquiti airMAX) employ a proprietary flavor of Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA). Unlike standard Wi-Fi, TDMA schedules precise time slots for each device, drastically reducing collisions, lowering latency, and ensuring fair bandwidth allocation for massive network scalability.

3. Advanced Protocols (Breaking the Limits)

  • Mimosa SRS: Optimized TDMA for superior interference rejection and an extended frequency range (4.9–6.4 GHz).
  • Ubiquiti LTU Technology: Custom silicon for high-capacity PtP backhaul links, achieving 1+ Gbps throughput with ultra-low latency.

My Role: Assistant Network Engineer — Core & Access

I specialize in the configuration and monitoring of our entire routing stack, including:

  • Core Management: Ensuring robust link performance and fault tolerance for our IIG and fiber backhaul using advanced protocols.
  • WISP Operations: Optimizing the wireless access layer, including link budget analysis and frequency planning.
  • System Configuration: Expertly configuring and diagnosing MikroTik RouterOS via Winbox across various network tiers.
  • Support & Deployment: Providing prompt, effective remote and on-site support, including hands-on troubleshooting and new infrastructure deployment.

Equipment Expertise

Our device inventory includes a mix of rugged, high-performance equipment:

  • Cambium ePMP Force 180/190/200: Subscriber Modules with excellent price-to-performance ratio, providing 200+ Mbps real-world throughput.
  • Ubiquiti PowerBeam M5: High-gain dish antenna for long-range links and superior noise immunity.
  • Mimosa C5x: Highly versatile and rugged with modular antenna system (up to 25 dBi) and extended frequency range.
  • Ubiquiti AirFiber 5X HD: High-capacity PtP backhaul utilizing custom LTU technology for multi-Gigabit capacity.
  • Ubiquiti NanoStation M5/M2: Compact form factor for shorter-range links or discreet installations.

Critical SharePoint Zero-Day Exploited: What You Need to Know About CVE-2025-53770

CVE-2025-53770 is likely one of the most critical SharePoint vulnerabilities to date.

On Sunday, July 20, Microsoft alerted customers to an actively exploited critical zero-day vulnerability, CVE-2025-53770, affecting SharePoint servers. SharePoint Server 2016, 2019, and the Subscription editions are all susceptible, while SharePoint Online is not affected. Any organizations running on-prem SharePoint are advised to act immediately.

Authentication is entirely bypassed by spoofing the Referer header, allowing attackers to upload files, including a web shell. In some cases, researchers are seeing .Net machine keys being stolen so attackers can maintain access even after patching.

Key Points

  • No login required: Anyone with access to the server can get in, even without a username or password.
  • Full control: Attackers can do anything the SharePoint server can do, including reading documents, uploading malware, or attacking other parts of your network.
  • Silent persistence: Even if you apply patches, attackers may still have access if they stole the server's keys.

How the Exploit Works

The vulnerability originated from bypassing mitigations for two previously patched bugs, CVE-2025-49704 and CVE-2025-49706. At the center of the exploit is the ToolPane.aspx endpoint. Attackers found it can be tricked into processing unauthorized requests by sending a spoofed Referer header claiming they're coming from SharePoint's logout page, SignOut.aspx.

Detection and Mitigation

Review your SharePoint logs and endpoint or SIEM alerts. Key indicators to look for include:

  • Suspicious requests to /ToolPane.aspx
  • Presence of a web shell (check for spinstall0.aspx)
  • Unusual server activity from w3wp.exe
  • Known attacker IPs: 107.191.58.76, 104.238.159.149, 96.9.125.147

Immediate Actions Required

  1. Apply Microsoft's latest security updates
  2. Enable AMSI and update antivirus
  3. Rotate your machine keys
  4. Isolate vulnerable servers if needed
  5. Reset credentials and review access
  6. Run threat hunts and EDR scans
  7. Check backups and logging
Source: This analysis is based on research by Dr. Johannes Ullrich, Dean of Research for SANS Technology Institute and founder of the Internet Storm Center (DShield.org).
